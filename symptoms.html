Symptoms of Dysfunctional Accesses

1. Changes in pulse or thrill

Once an internal vascular access is created you will notice a strong pulse and vibration in the access. You should get into the habit of checking for the presence of this pulse and vibration on a regular basis by performing a one-minute-examination of your access; by looking, feeling, and listening to your fistula or graft. The following definitions will assist you in performing and understanding this exam.

  • Thrill: a vibration or buzz in the full length of the access.
  • Pulse: slight beating like a heartbeat. Fingers placed lightly on the access should move slightly.
  • Pulsatile: The beat is stronger than a normal pulse.Fingers placed lightly on the access will rise and fall with each beat.

When you feel your access you want to notice a thrill and a pulse. The thrill and pulse should always feel the same. If your fistula or graft becomes "pulsatile" you should notify your dialysis team right away, as this may be an indication that something has changed in your access.

When you listen by placing your ear over the access you should become familiar with the sound that you hear. This sound should always be the same. If it changes or you hear no sound you should notify your dialysis team right away since this is another indication that something has changed in your access.

2. Swelling of your vascular access or the extremity

When you look at your vascular access the skin over your access is all one color and looks like the skin around it. If there is redness, swelling or drainage, or if there are skin bulges with shiny, bleeding, or peeling skin you should notify your dialysis team immediately. These are all indications that something has changed with your access.

3. Pain, Numbness, Tingling, or Coldness of the access or extremity

These are all signs that something is changing with your access and should be reported to your dialysis team right away.

All of the above are signs and symptoms that YOU should be aware of. The following are additional indications of dysfunction that your dialysis team will monitor

4. Difficulty cannulating (needling) your fistula or graft

 

5. Prolonged bleeding from your needle sites after hemodialysis

 

6. A pulsatile access

Symptoms of dysfunction of Peritoneal Dialysis(PD) Catheters

Peritoneal Dialysis is another treatment option for patients requiring dialysis. Unlike hemodialysis, PD is performed at home by you and in some cases a family member as a helper. The access does NOT require needles, or access to blood. PD uses blood vessels that line the inside of your belly (your peritoneum) as a filter to clean your blood. Your blood does not leave your body—so there are no needles. Instead, you put sterile fluid into your belly through a tube, or PD Catheter. A PD Catheter is a safe access. It is different from a hemodialysis catheter, which is risky.

The most frequent complication of a PD catheter is infection. Your PD catheter is a door into your body for germs. If you opt for PD as your treatment modality you will be fully trained by the PD nurse on how to avoid infection of the exit site (the point where the catheter leaves your body) or peritonitis (a painful infection caused by germs inside your belly). On occasion, your PD catheter may not fill or drain as well as it should. Any symptoms of peritonitis, catheter infection, or a malfunctioning PD catheter should be reported to the PD care team as soon as possible.
