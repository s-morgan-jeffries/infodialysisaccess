Do's and Don'ts

Caring for a fistula or graft

When caring for a fistula or graft, DO…
  • Wear a medical alert bracelet to notify healthcare providers that you have a hemodialysis vascular access, the location and type of access. Let any medical providers know that no blood pressures or vein punctures are to be done on your vascular access limb
  • Keep your access area clean and free of any trauma. Your dialysis care team will teach you how to carefully wash it before each treatment.
  • Look for signs of infection, including pain, tenderness, swelling or redness around your access area.
  • Be aware of any fever and flu-like symptoms. If you do get an infection and catch it early, it can usually be treated with antibiotics.
To protect your access from any restriction or trauma, DON'T…
  • wear tight clothes, jewelry or anything that puts pressure on your access
  • sleep on top of or rest on your access area
  • carry bags or heavy items across your access area
  • have blood drawn from your access arm
  • have blood pressure taken from your access arm
DO learn to feel the vibration of blood going through your access and,
  • Check it several times a day
  • Call your dialysis care team immediately if the flow stops or changes, as this could be a blood clot that has formed and needs to be treated.
DO learn to listen to the sound of blood flowing through your access:
  • If the sound changes, it could be an indication that blood vessels are narrowing
  • If the sound changes, it could be an indication that blood vessels are narrowing
DO prevent tearing or damage to your access by paying attention to the needle stick locations when you're being put on dialysis:
  • The arterial and venous needle tips should be at least two (2) inches apart from each other
  • The needles should be away from access surgical scars
  • The new needle stick sites should be at least ¼" from the sites used the time before
  • Allow about two (2) weeks for healing of previous sites before using them again to help maintain the health of the access.
DO ask your nephrologist and dialysis care team about numbing creams to reduce the pain of needle sticks.

Self-Cannulation

DO ask your care team about training for self-cannulation if you wish to control and participate in this part of your vascular care and treatment. Many patients find they prefer having control of the needle stick process (self-cannulation).

  • Your team will provide you with clean gloves and teach you the proper procedures to stop bleeding as well as prevent infection.

Caring for a Central Venous Catheter (CVC)

Your catheter should only be used by the dialysis staff.

When caring for a central venous catheter, DO:
  • Keep your catheter exit sign clean and dry. Catheter care and a dressing change should be performed at every hemodialysis treatment
  • Keep your dressing clean, dry, and securely intact between hemodialysis treatments.
  • Make sure that the catheter end caps remain in place, they should only be removed by the dialysis staff.
  • Make sure that the clamps on the catheter limbs remain closed at all times except during hemodialysis.
  • Report any indications of infection to your Dialysis Care team and your doctor immediately. Signs include redness, tenderness, an elevated temperature, or any blood or pus on the dressing site.
When caring for a central venous catheter, DON'T:
  • Remove the dressing
  • Get the dressing wet

Caring for a Peritoneal Dialysis Catheter (PD Catheter)

Your catheter is your lifeline. If you take care of it properly from the beginning, it is more likely that you will heal quickly and you will have a smooth transition into your life on peritoneal dialysis.

You will have a bandage on after surgery. When caring for your PD catheter, DO:
  • Talk to your nurse before you leave to schedule an appointment to change your bandage.
DON'Ts:
  • Do not change the bandage yourself. Your nurse will teach you the right way to care for your catheter. If the bandage is soaked through or becomes loose, you should call the Peritoneal Dialysis Staff
  • Do not take a shower or tub bath. If you shower or bathe before the skin around the catheter heals, it can lead to an infection. Your nurse will tell you when it is safe to take a shower or bath.
  • Constipation can be a problem after surgery. If your bowels don't move within 72 hours, you should call the Peritoneal Dialysis Unit. Do not take over the counter laxatives unless you have been instructed to do so
  • Do not lift any heavy objects. Your activities for the first few weeks after surgeryshould be light.
  • You should not pull on the catheter.
  • Your incision will be closed with sutures, which will need to be removed. If there are no problems, the nurse should be able to remove your sutures.
  • You will have some soreness and discomfort for the first few days, and your surgeon will probably prescribe a pain medication for you
  • It is important that you report any indications of infection to your Peritoneal Dialysis Nurse and your doctor immediately. Signs include redness, tenderness, an elevated temperature, or any blood or pus on the dressing site.
